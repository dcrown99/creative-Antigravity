# Statistical Pairs Trading & Cointegration in FX Markets
# Source: Compiled from quantinsti.com, academic research summaries

## History and Institutional Evidence (Grade A)

Pairs trading was first introduced in the mid-1980s by a group of quantitative researchers employed by Morgan Stanley (notably Nunzio Tartaglia's group). It remains one of the most widely used strategies in hedge funds and institutional quantitative finance.

Academic papers (Engle & Granger 1987, Johansen 1991) provide the theoretical foundation via cointegration theory. Multiple studies (EUR.NL, ResearchGate, SSRN) confirm profitability in FX markets over 10-year periods.

Key study result: A cointegration trading strategy applied to 27 currency exchange rate combinations found 23 cointegrated pairs, yielding an average realized return of 6.5% over a 10-year period (EUR.NL thesis).

## Alpha Source (Structural, NOT Temporary)

The alpha source is STRUCTURAL MEAN REVERSION grounded in:
1. **Macroeconomic linkages**: Currency pairs with shared economic drivers (commodity exporters AUD/NZD, European economic integration EUR/GBP) have fundamental reasons to co-move.
2. **Cointegration persistence**: Even if short-term deviations occur due to news/sentiment, the long-run equilibrium is restored by economic fundamentals.
3. **Market neutrality**: Long one currency, short another = hedged against overall market direction. HFTs compete on microseconds; this strategy exploits multi-day/week mean reversion that HFTs cannot profitably arbitrage.

NOT technical-indicator based: Entry is triggered by STATISTICAL DEVIATION (Z-score) from a fundamental long-run relationship, not by price patterns.

## Key Concepts

### Cointegration
- Two non-stationary time series whose linear combination IS stationary
- Identified by: Augmented Dickey-Fuller (ADF) test on the spread
- If p-value < 0.05 on ADF test → spread is stationary → pair is cointegrated
- Johansen test used for multivariate cointegration

### Spread Definition
```
Spread = log(Price_A) - n * log(Price_B)
```
Where `n` = hedge ratio, calculated by OLS regression of Price_A on Price_B.

### Z-score
```
Z = (Spread - rolling_mean(Spread, window)) / rolling_std(Spread, window)
```
- Measures how many standard deviations the spread is from its rolling mean
- New distribution has mean=0, std=1

## Concrete Entry/Exit Rules (Standard Academic Implementation)

### Entry:
- **LONG spread** (buy A, sell B): when Z-score < -2.0
  (spread is abnormally low → expect reversion upward)
- **SHORT spread** (sell A, buy B): when Z-score > +2.0
  (spread is abnormally high → expect reversion downward)

### Exit:
- **Close position**: when Z-score reverts to between -0.5 and +0.5
  (spread returned to near-equilibrium)
- **Stop Loss**: when |Z-score| > 3.0 (cointegration may have broken down)

### Parameters (tunable):
- Entry threshold: Z = ±2.0 (range: 1.5 to 2.5)
- Exit threshold: Z = ±0.5 (range: 0.0 to 0.5)
- Rolling window for Z-score: 20-60 bars (daily bars typical)
- ADF test lookback: 250-500 bars (1-2 years of daily data)

## Best FX Pairs for Cointegration

### Highest historical cointegration:
1. **EURGBP** - Both European, shared macro drivers, strong cointegration evidence
2. **AUDNZD** - Both commodity exporters, close economic ties
3. **EURUSD / GBPUSD basket** - USD leg creates co-movement
4. **AUDCAD** - Both commodity currencies (oil vs iron ore)

### Note on pair stability:
- AUD/NZD cointegration broke down post-2021 due to different COVID monetary policies
- EUR/GBP retains stronger long-run cointegration due to institutional ties (ECB/BOE interaction)
- Regime shifts (global crises, policy divergence) can break cointegration → requires rolling ADF re-test

## Implementation Approach for MT5 (Retail-Compatible)

### Adaptation for single-account FX:
Since MT5 retail accounts can't truly go "long one / short another" as a market-neutral portfolio in the traditional sense (we trade FX pairs, not individual currencies), implementation must:
1. Select a CROSS PAIR where the arbitrage is embedded (e.g., EURGBP = EUR/USD ÷ GBP/USD)
2. OR trade the SPREAD DIRECTLY on a correlated pair basket

### Simplified approach for MT5:
- Use EURGBP (or AUDNZD) as a single instrument
- Model its "fair value" using EURUSD and GBPUSD as reference
- Entry when EURGBP deviates significantly from implied cross rate
- Exit when it reverts

### Alternative: AUDNZD Z-score reversion
- Use AUDNZD directly as the spread instrument
- Calculate rolling Z-score of AUDNZD (it IS the spread between AUD and NZD)
- Enter when Z-score extreme, exit at mean reversion

## Backtest Expectations

- Trade frequency: 100-300 trades/year on H4 timeframe
- Average holding period: 1-5 days  
- Win rate: typically 60-70% (mean reversion strategies have high WR)
- Profit Factor: 1.5-2.5 in favorable regimes
- Key risk: cointegration breakdown during regime changes (requires stop-loss)

## Risk of Cointegration Breakdown
- 40% of historically cointegrated pairs lose cointegration in next period
- Mitigation: Rolling ADF test, re-validate every quarter
- Stop loss at |Z| > 3.0 prevents catastrophic loss on breakdown
